Cote d'Ivoire: Ouattara officially sworn in as president

By Peter Uduehi
 
Alassane Ouattara has been officially sworn in as president of Ivory Coast after months of unrest and wrangling brought on by former leader Laurent Gbagbo's refusal to step down when the results of the recent election reported that he had lost the poll.
Four months of fighting ensued between forces loyal to Gbagbo and those of Ouattara backed by the French and the United Nations forces. Gbagbo was eventually defeated after his capture in April. He and his wife were forced out of the presidential palace's bunker in Abidjan.
Since then Ouattara has called for calm and urged every Ivorian to desist from further violence and bloodshed saying Gbagbo will be granted a fair trial for his constitutional crimes. Thousands of people were reported killed in the four-month fighting. Observers say political discord in Ivory Coast is based on religious differences with the predominantly northern section favouring Ouattara and the predominantly Christian south supporting Gbagbo.
"I salute all the members of the Constitutional Council for the courageous decision that they have taken," Ouattara said after the ceremony held in a room in the presidential palace, scene of some of the worst fighting.
 
"It has re-established the integrity of the ballot box and given justice to the Ivorian people," he said. The swearing-in will be followed by a more lavish inauguration in the coming weeks, Ouattara's government has said.
 
The ceremony was conducted by Constitutional Council President Paul Yao N'Dre, a close Gbagbo ally who had sworn in the incumbent leader in late November after rejecting U.N.-certified results showing Ouattara won.
 
N'Dre, who retained his post at the top of the country's highest legal body despite Gbagbo's eventual defeat, urged Ivorians to forgive and reconcile their differences. He was heckled at times by members of the audience.
 
"Mr. President of the Republic, we are seeking the right words of compassion so that Ivorians and Ivory Coast can forgive in the way that they know how," he said.
 
The ceremony was attended by dignataries and members of the diplomatic corps, including the ambassador from former colonial power France, a far cry from Gbagbo's swearing-in in November attended his young Patriot youth militia.
